Indian Peafowl - Peacock

About the Indian Peafowl

The Indian Peafowl, also known as the **Peacock** (scientific name *Pavo cristatus*), is native to the Indian subcontinent. It is one of the most beautiful and recognizable birds in the world, famous for the male's spectacular tail feathers, which are used in courtship displays. These feathers are iridescent and contain a variety of colors, including blue, green, and gold, making the peacock a symbol of beauty and grace.

The peafowl is a ground-dwelling bird, and it is often seen foraging for seeds, fruits, and insects in the forest floor. Though the Indian peafowl is mostly known for its stunning appearance, it is also an important part of the ecosystem in its native regions, controlling insect populations and spreading seeds.

As the national bird of India, the peacock holds cultural and symbolic significance. It is revered in Hindu mythology and often associated with deities such as Saraswati and Kartikeya. Despite its beauty, the peafowl faces threats from habitat loss and hunting. However, it has been successfully protected within national parks and reserves, ensuring its continued presence in the wild.

  • Name: Pavo cristatus
  • Family: Phasianidae
  • Population: Stable in the wild (approximately 1.5 million individuals)
  • Kingdom: Indian Subcontinent
  • Habitat: Forests, grasslands, agricultural fields
  • Diet: Omnivorous (seeds, fruits, insects, small reptiles)
  • National Parks: Sariska National Park, Keoladeo National Park
  • Status in the Wild: Least Concern (LC)
