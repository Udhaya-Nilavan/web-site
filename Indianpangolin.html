Indian Pangolin

About the Indian Pangolin

The Indian pangolin (Manis crassicaudata) is a mammal native to the Indian subcontinent. Known for its tough, protective scales made of keratin, this nocturnal creature is a unique species of anteater. Pangolins, often referred to as "scaly anteaters," are the only mammals wholly covered in scales. The Indian pangolin primarily feeds on ants, termites, and other insects, using its long, sticky tongue to catch its prey.

Unfortunately, the Indian pangolin is critically endangered due to illegal poaching for its scales, which are used in traditional medicine and for other purposes. Habitat loss and human-wildlife conflict also pose significant threats to its survival. Conservation efforts are underway to protect the species, but more awareness and action are needed to ensure its future.

  • Name: Manis crassicaudata
  • Family: Manidae
  • Total Population: Estimated to be fewer than 1,000 individuals
  • Kingdom: Indian Subcontinent, Southeast Asia
  • Habitat: Tropical forests, grasslands, and savannas
  • National Parks: Bandipur National Park (India), Kaziranga National Park (India)
  • Status in the Wild: Critically Endangered
